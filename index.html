About This Project

The Corpus

125 Lincoln speeches were drawn from public domain sources spanning 1832 to April 1865 — from his first campaign broadside to his last public address three days before his assassination.

The corpus covers every major phase of his public life: Illinois legislator, one-term congressman, prairie lawyer, Senate candidate, presidential candidate, and wartime president.

The Question

How did Lincoln's language work on its audiences? What did it emphasize, what did it leave out, and what did it make easy to believe without argument?

These tools analyze his speeches computationally across four dimensions: context, rhetoric, audience adaptation, and argumentation. Each tool surfaces patterns at scale; each also identifies the limits of what scale alone can reveal.

A Note on Method

Every tool in this project uses computational detection — keyword scoring, pattern matching, formula-based readability scores — to surface patterns across a corpus too large to read all at once. These methods are genuinely useful for identifying what to look at more carefully. They are not substitutes for reading.

The most important findings here came from questioning the output: noticing that Flesch-Kincaid scores misrepresent 19th-century oratory, that Lincoln's greatest speeches are also his most self-effacing (the word “I” appears zero times at Gettysburg), and that his structural false dichotomies go undetected because they don't use the word “either.” The computational layer raised these questions. Reading answered them.
