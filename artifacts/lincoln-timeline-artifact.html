About This Analysis

The Corpus

125 speeches and addresses spanning Lincoln's public career from his first recorded address in 1832 to his last public speech on April 11, 1865 — three days before his assassination. The corpus includes debates, inaugural addresses, military orders, congressional messages, and occasional remarks.

Reading Level: A Caution

Flesch-Kincaid grade scores here range from 10 to 16 — upper high school to graduate level by modern standards. This reflects sentence length and syllable count, not audience sophistication.

Lincoln's audiences were trained for long-form oratory; the Lincoln-Douglas debates ran seven hours. High FK scores reveal the structure of 19th-century public address, not an elite audience.

What the Data Shows

Reading level rises through the pre-war years — Lincoln's arguments grow more elaborate — then drops sharply after Fort Sumter. Wartime rhetoric is compressed, declarative, and direct. The Second Inaugural reverses this trend: dense, philosophical, only 703 words.

What the Data Cannot Show

Metadata captures structure, not meaning. The Gettysburg Address scores modestly on complexity but is among the most carefully constructed speeches in American history. Numbers describe what the algorithm can count; close reading is still required to explain why those numbers are significant.
