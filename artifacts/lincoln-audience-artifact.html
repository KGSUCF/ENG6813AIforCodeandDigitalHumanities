The Gettysburg Address: A Pronoun Anomaly

The Gettysburg Address is the most extreme case in the corpus: we = 55.1 per 1,000 words, I = 0.0. Lincoln effaces himself entirely while speaking in the plural voice of the nation. This is not accidental — the speech is about collective sacrifice and shared purpose, not presidential authority.

Compare the Second Inaugural: I = 1.4, they = 2.8, we = 14.3. As the war nears its end, Lincoln allows a first-person voice to reappear, but still keeps himself subordinate to the collective we and the referenced they — the South — he is addressing indirectly.

These pronoun patterns were surfaced by computational analysis of the full corpus. They would be difficult to notice through reading alone, but they reward close attention once identified: they reveal the degree to which Lincoln's rhetoric is consistently oriented outward, toward the audience and the nation, rather than inward toward the speaker.

About This Analysis

What "Audience" Means Here

Speeches were categorized by their primary intended audience based on historical context: Congress (State of the Union messages, special messages), General Public (major addresses, inaugural speeches), Political Rally (campaign and party addresses), Military/Soldiers (orders, addresses to troops), and Civic Organization (dedications, society addresses).

Sentence Length as a Signal

Average sentence length is one of the most reliable stylistic signals in this corpus. Military addresses run short — Lincoln is giving orders or acknowledging service. Congressional messages run long — Lincoln is building legal arguments. The difference (21 vs. 40 words) is not about the complexity of Lincoln's thought; it is about the constraints of the communicative situation.

What Pronouns Reveal

Pronoun frequency is a blunt instrument, but the patterns here are striking. Lincoln's most celebrated speeches (Gettysburg, Second Inaugural) are also his most self-effacing: I is nearly absent. The speaker disappears into the collective. This is a deliberate rhetorical strategy, not a stylistic accident.

Limits of the Analysis

Audience categories simplify real complexity. The Gettysburg Address was delivered to soldiers, families, politicians, and journalists simultaneously. "General Public" covers speeches that ranged from massive outdoor rallies to small indoor meetings. Formality scores are derived from vocabulary patterns and may not capture the full range of register shifts within individual speeches.
