About This Analysis

How Reasoning Types Were Detected

Each speech was analyzed for keyword and phrase patterns associated with seven reasoning modes: moral (appeals to conscience, right/wrong), legal (constitutional argument, precedent), historical (reference to past events or figures), pragmatic (practical necessity, consequences), deductive (if/then logical chains), analogical (comparisons and metaphors), and inductive (evidence from examples).

Scores represent weighted keyword frequency per 1,000 words, not a holistic judgment of argumentative quality.

Key Findings

Moral reasoning dominates Lincoln's rhetoric in every era — but its character changes. Pre-war moral argument is theoretical and constitutional; wartime moral argument becomes existential and urgent.

Analogical reasoning (comparison, metaphor) declines sharply during the war. Lincoln stops explaining by comparison and starts declaring. The "house divided" metaphor belongs to a different Lincoln than the one who issued the Emancipation Proclamation.

What the Numbers Miss

The Gettysburg Address scores modestly on most reasoning types because it is short and abstract. Its power is not algorithmic. Pattern-matching counts occurrences of legal vocabulary or logical connectors — it cannot measure the weight of "Four score and seven years ago" as both historical invocation and moral indictment.

The Wartime Shift

The single most visible pattern in this dataset is the compression of Lincoln's argumentative repertoire after Fort Sumter. Pre-war Lincoln deploys all seven modes; wartime Lincoln concentrates on moral and pragmatic argument, often within the same short address. The war did not change what Lincoln believed — it changed what he needed to say, and how quickly he needed to say it.
