Moral vs. Legal Reasoning — Rolling Average Across Career

The central finding: Moral reasoning dominates throughout Lincoln's career, but its lead over legal reasoning widens during the war years. This is counterintuitive — one might expect a wartime president to lean more heavily on constitutional and legal justification. Instead, Lincoln increasingly argued from moral first principles, particularly after the Emancipation Proclamation. The legal case for emancipation was contested; the moral case was not. By the Second Inaugural, legal reasoning has almost vanished entirely.

Pragmatic Reasoning Over Time

War demands practical argument: Pragmatic reasoning — arguments from necessity, consequence, and policy effect — rises sharply during the wartime period. This reflects the changed demands on Lincoln as president: he was no longer debating abstract principles with Douglas but justifying specific decisions (military conscription, suspension of habeas corpus, emancipation as a war measure) to skeptical audiences. The arguments had to be about what would work, not only what was right.

The Shift in Lincoln's Argument Strategy

What changed — and what didn't: The most significant shift between Lincoln's pre-war and wartime reasoning is the rise of pragmatic argument (+13%) and the relative decline of analogical reasoning (−33%). Pre-war Lincoln argued by comparison and metaphor — the house divided, the machinery of slavery, the framed timbers of a conspiracy. Wartime Lincoln argued from necessity and effect. The poetic mode gave way to the executive one.
What stayed constant: Moral and legal reasoning hold nearly steady across the eras. Lincoln never abandoned the constitutional frame, even as he pushed it to its limits. And he never stopped making the moral argument, even when political prudence might have counseled silence on it. This consistency is itself a rhetorical strategy: it made him harder to characterize as either a radical (pure moralist) or an opportunist (pure pragmatist).

Methodological Note
Reasoning types were scored by counting indicator words and phrases in each speech text: moral reasoning by terms like “right,” “wrong,” “duty,” and “conscience;” legal reasoning by references to the Constitution, law, and jurisdiction; historical reasoning by references to the Founders and the founding period; pragmatic reasoning by terms of necessity, consequence, and policy; and so on. All scores are normalized per 1,000 words to allow comparison across speeches of very different lengths. The rolling averages on the timeline charts use a window of six speeches on each side to smooth short-term variation. These scores identify the presence of reasoning types, not their quality or persuasive success — a speech high in “moral” language may be making a powerful philosophical argument or simply invoking God as a rhetorical prop. The five spotlight speeches in the Era Comparison tab were selected to illustrate the arc of Lincoln's argumentative development across his career.
