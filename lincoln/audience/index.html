Key Metrics by Audience Type

Average Sentence Length (words)

Soldiers vs. Congress: Lincoln's speeches to soldiers averaged 21.8 words per sentence — roughly half the length of his addresses to the general public (40.8). For men about to return to battle, he stripped language to its core. Congressional messages, by contrast, built long sentences because they were written to be read, not heard, and needed legal precision.

Vocabulary Richness (type-token ratio)

What richness measures: Type-token ratio = unique words ÷ total words in the first 500. A higher score means less repetition. Lincoln's scores are remarkably consistent across audience types, suggesting his vocabulary range didn't change much — what changed was sentence structure and formality.

Reading Level (Flesch-Kincaid Grade) by Audience

A note on these scores: FK grade reflects sentence length and syllable count — not clarity or accessibility. Speeches to soldiers score lowest (9.1) because they are short and direct. Speeches to the general public score highest (14.4) largely because ceremonial addresses like the Gettysburg Address and both Inaugurals use long, complex sentences that are architecturally deliberate, not accidentally difficult. Political rally speeches (yellow) cluster in the middle, confirming Lincoln's middle-register approach to campaign audiences.

Pronoun Use by Audience (per 1,000 words)

Pronoun choice is one of the most reliable signals of how a speaker positions themselves relative to an audience. “We/our” signals solidarity and shared identity. “You/your” directly addresses the listener. “I” foregrounds the speaker's individual authority or experience. “They/their” defines an out-group.

The Pronoun Shift at Gettysburg

The Gettysburg Address is an outlier in every pronoun category. With we/our = 55.1 per thousand words and I = 0.0, it is Lincoln's most collectively-voiced speech in the entire corpus. He addresses a crowd of thousands on a battlefield where 51,000 men had become casualties four months earlier, and he uses the word “I” not once.

Compare this to his speeches to soldiers, where “I” averages 22.9 per thousand — the highest of any audience type. When addressing troops directly, Lincoln spoke personally and informally, often expressing his own feelings about the war. At Gettysburg, speaking to a national public audience in a ceremonial register, he erased himself entirely.

The Second Inaugural shows the same instinct at the war's close: “they” drops to 2.8 (its lowest in any major speech), and “I” nearly disappears at 1.4. By 1865, Lincoln had stopped naming enemies and stopped foregrounding himself. The grammar of the speech enacts its argument about national reconciliation before a word of the argument is made.

Methodological Note
Audience classifications were assigned algorithmically from speech titles and occasion keywords: speeches with “Congress,” “Senate,” or “Annual Message” were coded as Congressional; speeches to named regiments or troop assemblies as Military/Soldiers; campaign speeches and debates as Political Rally; and so on. This approach is imperfect — the Cooper Union address was classified as General Public, but its actual audience was New York Republican elites, a more specific group than the label suggests. Readability scores (Flesch-Kincaid) and sentence length were calculated from the raw text. Pronoun counts include all forms of each pronoun (we/us/our, you/your, etc.). All metrics are computed from the same 125-speech corpus as the other tools in this project.
